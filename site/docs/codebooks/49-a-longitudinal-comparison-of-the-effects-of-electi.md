## Description

In 2024, citizens worldwide voted amid concerns about democratic backsliding, raising questions about how election outcomes shape system legitimacy and governmental distrust. We examined these processes and the moderating role of electoral trust using three-wave panel data from the UK (Study 1; N = 604; 1,677 observations) and US (Study 2; N = 512; 1,344 observations) general elections. For electoral winners in both countries, system legitimization increased and governmental distrust decreased. Electoral losers’ responses diverged: UK right-leaning losers remained stable in both variables, whereas US left-leaning losers became more distrustful and showed lower system legitimization, reflecting potential heightened polarization before electoral trust was accounted for.
